1. Title
- I Was Part of a Search Team For a Missing Child. Something Else Was in Those Woods.

2. Main Characters
- Sawyer Santos — narrator; Pinewood National Park ranger (protagonist).
- Scott Wagner — Sawyer’s partner, fellow ranger.
- Sheri Ellis, Beau (Bo) Salinas — fellow rangers/searchers.
- Linda (Dispatch) — radio dispatcher who initiates the search.
- Melvin Strickland — missing seven-year-old boy.
- Dominic and Cassie Strickland — Melvin’s parents.
- Harold Jenkins — retired ranger who previously experienced similar phenomena.
- Marcus Dawson, Alan Cooper — past rangers mentioned in archival research (their cases mirror Sawyer’s).
- Supporting: sheriff’s deputies, volunteers, park personnel, unnamed trapped voices/souls.

3. Setting
- Pinewood National Park — primarily wooded trails: North Ridge Trail, South Trail, Pinewood Overlook.
- An impossible circular clearing deep in the woods (locus of supernatural events).
- Ranger station/headquarters, main trailhead parking lot.
- Donna’s Diner in Riverdale (for Harold Jenkins meeting).
- The Strickland family home and neighborhood.
- Timeframe: early autumn; action spans night searches, subsequent days, and a climactic return at night.

4. Narrative Structure
- Exposition
  - Sawyer is awakened by dispatch about Melvin’s disappearance; quick, professional mobilization of ranger teams; begins organized search.
  - Establishes setting, teams, weather (rapid temperature drop) and stakes (small child at night).
- Rising Action
  - Search progresses: discovery of red backpack, child’s belongings, and small footprints leading off-trail.
  - Sawyer and Scott follow erratic tracks into increasingly unnatural forest conditions (deepening cold, absolute silence).
  - Sawyer discovers a clearing and what he perceives as Melvin’s dead body with a pulsing blue light; he is pulled into a void of voices.
- Climax
  - The void experience: Sawyer’s consciousness separated; voices of the dead (including his parents and Scott) plead; Sawyer is nearly consumed but is violently yanked back into his body while Scott performs CPR.
  - Contradictory physical reality: other searchers report Sawyer walked, led teams to a different (alive) Melvin, and that Sawyer was found unresponsive — creating paradox and doubt.
- Falling Action
  - Sawyer’s internal unraveling: he researches archival cases, meets Harold Jenkins who confirms the pattern and warns him.
  - He surveils the Strickland family, grows convinced the returned Melvin is an impostor.
- Resolution
  - Sawyer returns deliberately to the clearing at night, tethered with a rope, enters the void intentionally, finds the trapped souls, locates the real Melvin, fights shadowy forces, and pulls the boy back into the physical world.
  - Reunites the real Melvin with his parents at dawn. Sawyer survives but is permanently changed; the woods remain dangerous and secretive.

5. Key Plot Points with timestamp ranges (approximate; total duration 96:16)
- 00:00–03:00 — Wake-up call and dispatch; Sawyer prepares emergency pack and leaves cabin.
- 03:00–12:00 — Arrival at main trailhead; briefing, team assignments, weather/stakes established.
- 12:00–30:00 — North Ridge Trail search begins; eerie environmental anomalies (cold, silence).
- 30:00–38:00 — Discovery of red backpack (toy truck, crackers, juice); footprints leading off-trail.
- 38:00–50:00 — Follow footprints to impossible circular clearing; Sawyer sees a motionless Melvin surrounded by dark liquid and glowing blue light; Sawyer pulled into void (first major confrontation).
- 50:00–56:00 — Sawyer’s violent return to his body while Scott is performing CPR; confusion over what happened; no physical evidence of the corpse in clearing.
- 56:00–66:00 — Return to trailhead: Melvin is present, unharmed; public “rescue” and thanks; Sawyer’s dissonance and decision to omit supernatural details from report.
- 66:00–78:00 — Sawyer researches park records; finds three analogous historical cases; calls and meets Harold Jenkins at Donna’s Diner; Harold confirms recurring pattern and warns Sawyer.
- 78:00–84:00 — Sawyer surveils the Strickland family; observes odd behaviors in the child who’s living with them; internal escalation of fear and urgency.
- 84:00–94:00 — Sawyer returns to the clearing at night with camera, rope, and tether; sets up; enters void deliberately with rope anchor; confronts voices and shadow-figures; finds and identifies the real Melvin; struggles to extract him.
- 94:00–96:16 — Intensive escape through collapsing/shifting woods; Sawyer brings real Melvin to the Stricklands at dawn; reunion; Sawyer survives but remains haunted; implication that the woods remain a trap.

6. Emotional Arc
- Professional alertness → focused urgency: Sawyer begins the search with calm competence and responsibility.
- Unease → mounting dread: environmental anomalies (cold, silence) and erratic tracks create growing anxiety.
- Terror and disorientation: discovery of the corpse and being pulled into the void; voices tug at Sawyer’s sense of self; temptation and grief (family voices) intensify vulnerability.
- Despair → resistance: overwhelmed by multiplicity of demands in the void but summons will to resist (anchor: duty to Scott and child).
- Confusion and guilt: upon return, Sawyer experiences cognitive dissonance — public “success” but private knowledge of an unresolved trapped soul; growing obsession.
- Isolation and obsession: research, surveillance, and sleep deprivation deepen loneliness and paranoia.
- Determination and sacrifice: Sawyer chooses to return, tethered, to extract the real child; faces shadowy resistance.
- Relief and fragile closure: rescue and reunification at dawn bring relief and validation, but a lingering haunted awareness remains — survivor’s burden.

7. Themes and Motifs
- Duty vs. Temptation — the ranger’s professional obligation drives him into moral choices; voices exploit love and grief to lure rescuers.
- Liminal spaces and the forest as a portal — the clearing functions as a threshold between worlds (the living and trapped souls).
- Identity and imposture — the idea of something returning that “looks” like a missing person but isn’t (doppelgänger / vessel motif).
- Grief and manipulation — dead/lost loved ones’ voices are used as bait; personal histories (Sawyer’s deceased parents) are exploited.
- Repetition and cyclical horror — similar cases across decades suggest an ongoing, generational pattern that the park conceals.
- Silence, cold, and blue light — sensory motifs used to signal the supernatural: unnatural silence, temperature drops, and the pulsing bluish luminescence from mouths.
- Anchors and tethers — the rope and radio represent connections to the physical world and sanity; cutting or reinforcing ties affects survival.
- Memory, perception, and official record vs. private truth — Sawyer must choose between filing an official, sanitized report and preserving the messy truth he experienced.
- The price of knowledge — learning the pattern isolates Sawyer and burdens him with ongoing responsibility; knowledge does not equate to relief.
- Rescue as moral imperative — a repeated emphasis on rescuers’ responsibility; the narrative interrogates what rescue means when the rescued are not whole or not who they appear to be.

Additional notes (narrative devices and structural observations)
- The story alternates conventional search-and-rescue procedure with escalating uncanny phenomena, grounding the supernatural in professional, procedural detail to heighten plausibility.
- The void sequence functions as the psychic core: it forces the protagonist to confront personal grief, duty, and temptation while revealing the larger pattern of the park’s hidden danger.
- Harold Jenkins operates as the archetypal elder/mentor who offers corroboration and an ominous warning, linking Sawyer’s experience to a historical pattern and imparting stakes.
- The final act reframes Sawyer from a passive victim of the phenomenon to an active rescuer who uses knowledge and preparation (rope tether) to rescue the actual child, giving him partial moral restitution but not full peace.
